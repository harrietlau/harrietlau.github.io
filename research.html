Research Themes

Tidal Tomography

The density field of the mantle is a key parameter in constraining the dynamics of mantle flow. We have set out to understand mantle density by appealing to a much-neglected geophysical process, namely solid Earth tides. Predicting the deformation of the solid Earth in response to periodic luni-solar forcings has a rich history in classical physics, with theoretical work dating back to Newton. The advent of modern space geodetic methods (e.g., surveying using the Global Positioning System) has yielded unprecedented constraints on solid Earth tidal deformation and revealed geographical variations in body tide deformation at the sub-mm level. These variations are indicative of heterogeneity in mantle buoyancy and elastic structure.

Using a tidal theory we developed, we have shown that the deepest parts of the mantle beneath African and the Pacific are characterized by negative buoyancy. Such a result holds implications for the compositional distribution of the mantle, which in turn provides information on the primordial state of the mantle, and its ongoing evolution and how efficiently the mantle mixes. This initial application shows the great promise of “tidal tomography” in elucidating deep mantle buoyancy structure and there are many directions to be taken (e.g., augmenting data to include many types of tides, joint inversions with more conventional data, further generalizing the theory to allow for more complicated processes).

Mantle viscosity across frequency

The Earth is subject to forces with timescales that range from milliseconds to billions of years, and the rheological response to these processes varies dramatically across this range: e.g., seismic waves triggered by brittle failure of rocks along faults are a transient and largely elastic phenomenon. In contrast, temperature differences of ~3000 K across the mantle have existed since the formation of Earth 4.5 billion years ago, driving a slow viscous convection that powers plate tectonics. At intermediate timescales of thousands to tens of thousands of years, the growth and disappearance of ice sheets over ice age cycles have led to viscoelastic perturbations in Earth’s shape, gravitational field and rotation axis that involve a rapid, elastic component and a slow, viscous component that persists to the present day. Yet all these processes transpire on the same Earth.

That the Earth can respond in such disparate ways to different geological processes is well known, and it has become increasingly evident that the treatment of these processes in isolation has led to a number of inconsistencies in our understanding of solid Earth structure and deformation. Such inconsistency may indicate that the different processes are sampling fundamentally different regimes of viscous flow or they may reflect the wide range of geological settings in which these processes occur. Is it possible to resolve this inconsistency and develop a more generalized model of Earth rheology? Our aim is to try just that, and requires the ability to model many processes with sufficient rheological flexibility while accounting for the spatial and temporal range of disparate geological and experimental observations.

Relevant Publications: Jackson & Faul (2010), Bellis & Holtzman (2014), Lau et al. (2016), Lau et al. (2017a)

Collaborators: Ben Holtzman (Columbia University; NY, USA), Cédric Bellis (Laboratoire de Mécanique et d'Acoustique, CNRS; France), David Al-Attar (Cambridge University; UK), Uli Faul (MIT; MA, USA)


Mantle viscosity across space

The 3-D elastic structure of the Earth was first revealed in the pioneering studies of seismic tomography in the early 1980s. In the intervening decades the resolution of these early images has improved considerably. However, the current state of knowledge of the Earth’s interior is fundamentally incomplete because 3-D variations in mantle viscosity, which likely reach several orders of magnitude, remain largely unconstrained. To improve this state of understanding, we are developing new theoretical and numerical techniques (namely, Adjoint Methods) for the relevant geodynamical processes (e.g., mantle convection, glacial isostatic adjustment) with the intention of making the modeling of these processes more computationally feasible. This effort is being led by collaborators at Cambridge University (Ophelia Crawford, David Al-Attar, and Jacky Austermann) and these new techniques have the promise to provide a tractable means to analyze mantle viscosity with an unprecedented level of quantitative analysis. The long term goal is to produce the first ever map of 3-D variations in viscosity which will provide extraordinary insight into the physical and chemical state of the Earth’s mantle.

Relevant Publications: Latychev et al. (2005), Al-Attar & Tromp (2013), Lau et al. (2016), Crawford et al. (2017)

Collaborators: Jacky Austermann (Columbia University; NY, USA), Ophelia Crawford (Cambridge University; UK), David Al-Attar (Cambridge University; UK), Konstantin Latychev (University of Toronto; Canada)


Planetary Tides

Planetary science has recently received much attention with new discoveries emerging from missions within our solar system (e.g., Curiosity, JUNO) or beyond (e.g., Kepler). These discoveries challenge entrenched views on planetary evolution and structure given the extreme parameter space in which planets and exoplanets exist. With ongoing and upcoming missions (e.g., GRAIL, Insight), tidal observations have the potential to provide significant constraints on planetary structure.

A natural extension of the work described above will be to generalize the theoretical tidal framework to accommodate arbitrary planetary structure (e.g., planets with purely solid cores like Mercury or extensive subsurface lakes like Europa). In many cases, tidal dissipation can be the dominant source of heat on other planetary bodies (e.g., the inner Jovian moons) and tidal measurements are the only constraints available on the density, elastic and viscous structure of planetary bodies. Exploiting such information can deepen our understanding of the body’s current and past thermal state. With more accurate tidal data becoming available from the GRAIL and (soon) Insight missions, generalizing and applying the theoretical and numerical methods developed will provide a timely research target.

Relevant Publications: Latychev et al. (2008), Zhong et al. (2012), Qin et al. (2014), Lau et al. (2017)

Collaborators: Chuan Qin (Harvard University; MA, USA), David Al-Attar (Cambridge University; UK)


Global Change

The overarching goal of the research themes listed above is to improve our understanding of how the solid Earth deforms over many spatial and temporal scales. If this is sucessful, such work will open up new collaborative opportunities that straddle multiple fields, combining mantle flow simulations with long term variations in geochemical cycles, atmospheric evolution, paleo and modern climate studies.

Examples include: understanding the present day mass balance of Antarctic ice sheet loss by incorporating the latest ideas of ice melt coupled with complex subsurface structure; exploring whether the possible periodicity of large-scale volcanism over millions of years reflects characteristic time scales in the mantle and the implications this holds for extinctions recorded in paleontological fossil records; how changes in Earth's rotation rate and continental configuration might affect tidal dissipation through time; how solid Earth deformation can help date the Black Sea flood (attributed to be the flood associated with Noah's ark).

Relevant Publications: Rohde & Muller (2005), Goldberg et al. (2016), Hay et al. (2016)

Collaborators: Jerry X. Mitrovica (Harvard University; MA, USA), Jacky Austermann (Columbia University; NY, USA), Carling Hay (Boston College; MA, USA), Andy Knoll (Harvard University; MA, USA), Brian Arbic (University of Michigan; MI, USA), Sam Goldberg (MIT; MA, USA).
